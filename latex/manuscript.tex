\begin{abstract}
We report the vetting of four high-priority transiting exoplanet candidates identified in TESS Full Frame Image (FFI) data: TIC 258285711, TIC 319431206, TIC 420914536, and TIC 432121978. Using a dual-layer verification pipeline incorporating statistical modeling via \texttt{TRICERATOPS}, kinematic transit duration constraints, and Lomb-Scargle out-of-transit stellar variability checks (SWEET test), we classify their natures. Two targets, TIC 258285711 and TIC 319431206, are retired as astrophysical false positives due to severe circular orbit duration violations and phase-locked rotational harmonics. Conversely, TIC 420914536 and TIC 432121978 are validated as highly resilient Ultra-Short-Period (USP) planet candidates ($P < 1$ day) passing all kinematic criteria. We recommend immediate ground-based radial velocity follow-up for these two viable planet candidates.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS; Ricker et al. 2015) has detected thousands of planet candidates. However, the large $21''$ pixel scale of the TESS cameras often results in transit signals contaminated by nearby astrophysical false positives, such as blended eclipsing binaries (BEBs) or stellar variability. Automated, multi-tiered vetting pipelines are essential to optimize ground-based follow-up resources (e.g., TFOP) by efficiently filtering out these false alarms. This Note details the systematic vetting of four candidates to isolate true planets from false positives using a combination of Bayesian probability modeling and physical orbital diagnostics.

\section{Methodology}
We performed a multi-tiered vetting analysis on the four targets using the latest available TESS Full Frame Image (FFI) light curves downloaded via \texttt{lightkurve}. For each target, the most recent sector was selected (Sectors fetched via the Quick-Look Pipeline, QLP, or the Science Processing Operations Center, SPOC).

Our pipeline evaluated the candidates across three distinct filters:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Statistical False Positive Modeling}: We deployed the Bayesian vetting framework \texttt{TRICERATOPS} (Giacalone et al. 2021) to calculate the False Positive Probability (FPP) and Nearby False Positive Probability (NFPP). The code queries Gaia DR3 to simulate local background stellar populations and assess the probability of background eclipsing binaries (BEBs) or nearby eclipsing binaries (NEBs) duplicating the observed signal.
    \item \textit{Kinematic Duration Constraints}: Assuming a stable circular orbit ($e=0$) and a central transit chord ($b=0$), we calculated the maximum theoretical transit duration ($T_{\text{max}}$) using Kepler's Third Law:
    $$a = \left(\frac{G M_* P^2}{4\pi^2}\right)^{1/3}$$
    $$T_{\text{max}} = \frac{2 R_*}{\left(\frac{2\pi a}{P}\right)}$$
    Stellar parameters ($M_*$ and $R_*$) were retrieved from the ExoFOP TESS Input Catalog (TIC v8.2). An observed transit duration $T_{\text{obs}}$ exceeding $T_{\text{max}}$ by more than a 15\% physical margin indicates an unphysical orbital velocity for a planet around the target star, serving as a signature of a blended binary system.
    \item \textit{Stellar Variability Check (SWEET Test)}: We applied a Lomb-Scargle periodogram analysis to the out-of-transit data to identify dominant stellar rotation or pulsation periods ($P_{\text{rot}}$). Signals where $P_{\text{rot}}$ matches a phase-locked harmonic or sub-harmonic of the orbital period ($P_{\text{orb}} \approx 0.5$, $1.0$, or $2.0 \times P_{\text{rot}}$) indicate that the periodic dip is caused by stellar activity (e.g., starspots) rather than an occulting companion.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Results and Discussion}
The execution logs and metrics from our vetting pipeline are compiled in Table 1.

The longer-period candidates both triggered critical physical violations. TIC 258285711 ($P = 7.5101$ d) failed the kinematic check with an observed duration of 4.08 hours against a physical maximum of 2.83 hours. Furthermore, the SWEET test revealed a dominant stellar variability period of exactly 15.00 days, establishing a phase-locked sub-harmonic ($2 \times P_{\text{orb}}$) driven by stellar rotation. The statistical analysis yielded a high $\text{FPP} = 0.7500$ (with $\text{NFPP} = 0.0000$), further corroborating the false positive classification. TIC 319431206 ($P = 7.0008$ d) similarly exhibited a highly inflated transit duration ($T_{\text{obs}} = 4.14$ hours vs. $T_{\text{max}} = 2.35$ hours) and a corresponding $\text{FPP} = 0.7500$, strongly implying a background eclipsing binary leaking flux into the aperture. We formally retire both targets as false positives.

Conversely, the ultra-short-period (USP) candidates, TIC 420914536 ($P = 0.7364$ d) and TIC 432121978 ($P = 0.7123$ d), passed all vetting criteria. Their observed durations ($0.67$ hours and $0.58$ hours, respectively) are strictly bounded by their theoretical kinematic limits ($1.29$ hours and $1.10$ hours), consistent with valid, high-impact parameter ($b \approx 0.85$) planet transits. No phase-locked stellar periodicities were detected.

Given their short orbital periods and physical consistency, TIC 420914536 and TIC 432121978 are highly resilient USP planet candidates. We present these dispositions to the community to optimize follow-up efficiency and suggest immediate high-precision radial velocity measurements to confirm their masses.
